Why Designing?

It all kicked off in my second year of college, the moment I got my very first laptop. I still remember scrolling through YouTube when I randomly heard someone say, "You can inspect websites and mess around with them." That instantly sparked my curiosity.

So I opened up the Google homepage, right-clicked, and hit Inspect. A whole new world opened up. I started tweaking colors, shifting margins, stretching buttons, changing text. Suddenly, I wasn’t just browsing a site — I was breaking it apart and building it back, one style at a time. And it was fun. Like, really fun. That’s when it hit me. This is my thing. This is where I belong.

And maybe the universe caught the vibe, because not long after, a friend called me about an internship that combined design and development. I jumped in, started designing real screens, building stuff from scratch, and soaking up everything like a sponge.

From that first inspect on Google to crafting full-fledged interfaces, it’s been a wild and exciting ride. No complaints. Just the pure joy of creating, exploring, and watching ideas come to life on screen. I’ve been hooked ever since. 😊

Challenges & How I Overcame Them

Turning obstacles into opportunities through creativity, calm, and clarity.

Working directly with marketing VPs, designers, and developers meant dealing with a lot of dynamic requests, often requiring out-of-the-box implementations. One recurring challenge was post-live issues due to clients not performing a hard refresh after deployment.

I remember one incident where a page went live, and soon after, there was panic. The client raised concerns saying "everything looks broken," with misaligned elements and visuals not rendering correctly. I stayed calm, assessed the situation quickly, and explained to both the team and the client that the issue was due to cached data and could be fixed with a hard refresh. The problem was resolved immediately, and I was appreciated for my timely response and clear communication.

Even if something was beyond what the designers could implement, I would guide them and explain, “You can actually do this and that.” I made sure they knew what was possible and how to approach it, helping turn ideas into reality even when they felt out of scope.

Another challenge I faced was when the development heads asked me to research a backend-related topic for the entire site. They said, "You can help with this." I had never worked on backend tasks before, but I didn’t say no. Instead, I saw it as a sign that they trusted my thinking and valued my input.

I spent time diving into the topic, did thorough research, and created a clear, concise report outlining the issue and how it could be solved. I overcame the challenge by believing in myself and the quality of my work. I was later appreciated for the detailed and thoughtful research I presented.
